## Overview
My communication style is thoughtful, structured, and practical. I tend to value clarity over performance, substance over excess, and usefulness over unnecessary polish. I generally prefer communication that helps move understanding forward rather than communication that is vague, inflated, or overly abstract.

I respond well to communication that is organized, grounded, and easy to work with. I tend to prefer material that is clear enough to act on, but not so stripped down that nuance is lost.

## How I Tend to Communicate
I naturally communicate by:
- organizing ideas into usable structure
- clarifying what is being asked or what is actually needed
- translating complexity into language other people can work with
- refining wording until it better reflects reality
- looking for the most coherent and practical articulation of a point

My style is usually more deliberate than flashy. I tend to care about precision, framing, and whether the communication actually helps the next step happen.

## What I Value in Communication
I value communication that is:
- grounded in reality
- concise without being thin
- thoughtful without being overcomplicated
- useful for decision-making or forward movement

I appreciate communication that respects both clarity and nuance. I usually want the point, the structure, and the practical implication.

## Preferred Tone
My preferred tone is:
- professional but human
- clear but not cold
- concise but not abrupt
- thoughtful but not overly academic
- calm, credible, and practical

In many contexts, I seem to prefer what could be described as a modern business tone: polished enough to be credible, but plain enough to still sound real.

## What Helps Communication Work Well
Communication tends to work best for me when:
- the purpose is clear
- the information is organized logically
- the language reflects reality rather than spin
- recommendations are grounded and practical
- unnecessary filler is removed
- the next step is visible
- important distinctions are made clear

I work especially well with communication that reduces confusion, highlights the signal, and makes the material easier to evaluate or use.

## What Tends Not to Work Well
Communication tends to work poorly for me when it is:
- vague
- overly performative
- cluttered
- inflated
- needlessly repetitive
- abstract without practical grounding
- polished in a way that obscures meaning
- rushed to the point that important distinctions disappear

I am less responsive to communication that sounds impressive but does not actually say much.

## Collaboration Preferences
In collaborative work, I tend to prefer:
- iterative refinement over one-shot output
- strong first drafts that can be sharpened
- clear options when choices need to be made
- honest assessment over forced optimism
- practical recommendations over generic advice
- communication that adapts to the task rather than forcing one fixed tone

I often work best when ideas can be developed through review, tightening, and clarification rather than pressure to get to a final version too quickly.

## Drafting Preferences
When developing written material, I tend to prefer:
- clean structure
- readable formatting
- language that sounds natural rather than overengineered
- strong summary framing
- wording that reflects operating reality, not just appearances
- materials that can be reused, adapted, or repurposed across contexts

I often value a draft that is structurally strong and easy to refine more than one that is overly finished but harder to work with.

## Notes
A recurring communication preference is that I do not just want words to sound better — I want them to become more accurate, more useful, and more aligned. Good communication should reduce friction, not add to it.

## Update Guidance
Update this file when communication preferences, tone preferences, or drafting preferences materially shift.
